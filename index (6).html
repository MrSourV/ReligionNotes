01 Creeds
Creed
A creed is a summary of the principal articles of faith professed by a church or community of believers. In other words, it outlines the main beliefs of a particular religion or group.
The Two Creeds
Note: Both creeds are structured around the Trinity.
  • Nicene Creed
  • Apostles' Creed — The summary of the beliefs of Jesus' chosen followers. On the day of Pentecost, the apostles composed the Apostles' Creed under the guidance of the Holy Spirit.
⚠ Exam Point

Doxology is a liturgical formula of Praise to God.

Apostles' Creed — Narrative Structure

The Apostles' Creed is a narrative that tells the gospel's story, beginning with the one true God — one who is self-sufficient.

The Holy Spirit

The Holy Spirit is the third person of the triune Godhead and does not appear until the latter portion of the creed. The confession of the Spirit coincides narratively with the sending of the Spirit at Pentecost, after the Ascension of Christ.

  • The Spirit empowers the gospel of Christ to build the church
  • Unites the saints in their spirits
  • Saves the lost
The Ending of the Creed

Finally, the creed ends with the new beginning — when the dead in Christ are raised incorruptible and the Lord's return ushers in the eternal joy of the new heaven and the new earth.

"The life everlasting" corresponds to Jesus renewing all things — not simply our receiving a ticket to heaven when we die.
02 Crusades
The Call to Crusade

Pope Urban II, seeing a way to harness the violent energy of Christian knights, made a speech calling for them to take back Jerusalem at the Council of Clermont in November, 1095 AD. Thousands responded, resulting in the First Crusade.

Political Motivations

There were political motivations for the Pope to get the Eastern Church to acknowledge him as the head of the whole church.

⚠ Test — Matching

Matching crusades will appear on the test. Be prepared to match each crusade with its key details.
